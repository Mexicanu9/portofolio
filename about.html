World Show.

Tour around the Globe.

Exploring the world history for Computer Science in a fun way! Showing you where it all happened and events that led to this technologies that we now have.

1936-1939: Turing's Theoretical Leap

Before the "Bombe" existed, Alan Turing moved from Cambridge University, where he conceived the Universal Turing Machine, to Bletchley Park. This transition from theoretical mathematics to the practical, high-stakes world of wartime codebreaking changed history. At Bletchley, he revolutionized the "Enigma" decryption process, turning a quiet country estate into the most important intelligence site in the world.

1945: The ENIAC Giant

At the University of Pennsylvania in Philadelphia, the ENIAC was born. It was a massive 30-ton machine that proved electronic computing was faster than any human "computer." It laid the groundwork for all modern hardware, though programming it required physically re-plugging cables and flipping thousands of switches by hand.

1952: Grace Hopper & The First Compiler

Admiral Grace Hopper traveled from her research at Harvard University in Cambridge, MA, to Philadelphia to work on the UNIVAC. It was here she developed the first compiler, A-0. She famously argued that "code should be written in English," leading directly to the creation of COBOL, the language that still powers much of the world's banking systems today.

1969: Apollo 11 - To the Moon

Software engineering as a discipline was pioneered by Margaret Hamilton. Her team at MIT in Cambridge, MA, developed the onboard flight software for the Apollo missions. This code traveled with the astronauts to Houston, Texas (NASA), and eventually to the Moon. Her "priority displays" saved the Apollo 11 landing when the computer became overloaded just minutes before touchdown.

1973: The Mouse & The GUI

In Palo Alto, California, the Xerox PARC team invented the "Alto." This was the first computer to use a mouse and a graphical interface. It represented the moment computers stopped being tools for scientists and started being tools for everyone, inspiring both Apple and Microsoft in the years to follow.

1990: Connecting the Planet

Tim Berners-Lee, working at CERN in Geneva, Switzerland, created the World Wide Web. By combining the existing Internet with hypertext, he allowed information to be shared globally and freely. This was the final step in turning a collection of isolated machines into a single, global "World Show."
